By Inspiration Source

🐝

Swarm Intelligence

Swarm intelligence models the collective behaviour of decentralised, self-organised agents. Each agent follows simple local rules — no central controller exists — yet the group as a whole exhibits intelligent search behaviour. Ants deposit pheromone on paths they traverse; shorter routes accumulate more pheromone due to faster round-trips, creating positive feedback that converges on efficient tours (ACO, Dorigo et al. 1996). Birds in a flock each track their personal best position and the flock’s global best, blending personal memory with social information to swarm toward optima (PSO, Kennedy & Eberhart 1995). Salps form transparent chains in the ocean, where the leader tracks the food source and followers average their position with the predecessor, propagating movement through the chain (SSA, Mirjalili et al. 2017).

🦈

Predator-Prey Dynamics

These algorithms model the tactics predators use to track, encircle, and capture prey. Grey wolves hunt in a strict alpha-beta-delta-omega hierarchy: the three fittest wolves guide the rest of the pack to surround prey from multiple directions (GWO, Mirjalili et al. 2014). Harris hawks cooperate in a surprise pounce strategy, alternating between perching to scout, chasing, and executing rapid Levy-flight dives when prey tries to escape (HHO, Heidari et al. 2019). Marine predators vary their foraging style based on the velocity ratio between predator and prey: Brownian exploration at high speeds, Levy flight exploitation at low speeds, with Fish Aggregating Devices adding random perturbation (MPA, Faramarzi et al. 2020). Humpback whales trap krill by blowing bubbles in a shrinking spiral, modelled as a logarithmic helix that tightens around the best solution (WOA, Mirjalili & Lewis 2016).

Evolutionary Computation

Rooted in Darwinian natural selection: a population of candidate solutions evolves over generations through selection, recombination, and mutation. Genetic Algorithms (Holland 1975) encode solutions as chromosomes. Tournament selection picks the fittest parents; ordered crossover (OX1) recombines their genetic material; swap mutation introduces random diversity. Elitism guarantees the best individual survives. Differential Evolution (Storn & Price 1997) mutates by adding a scaled difference vector between two random individuals to a third, then uses binomial crossover to combine with the target. Greedy selection keeps only improvements. DE excels on continuous parameter spaces and is one of the most competitive algorithms on standard benchmarks.

Biological / Organic

These algorithms draw on unique biological survival strategies that fall outside classical swarm or predator categories. The slime mould Physarum polycephalum builds near-optimal transport networks between food sources by strengthening veins along productive paths and pruning weak ones. In SMA (Li et al. 2020), agents are ranked by fitness: top-half agents approach the food source with amplified weights, while bottom-half agents explore randomly. Hummingbirds are the only birds that can hover and fly backwards, enabling precise nectar foraging. AHA (Zhao et al. 2022) models three strategies: guided foraging toward the best source, territorial foraging via diagonal flights, and long-range Levy-flight migration when local sources are depleted. A visit table tracks source frequencies to prevent revisiting.

By Role in Dual-Loop Solver

Outer-Loop (Tour Ordering)

The outer loop solves the combinatorial sequencing subproblem: in what order should the drone visit N sensor nodes to minimise total flight distance? Solutions are permutations of node indices. Algorithms use operators suited to discrete spaces — pheromone-weighted probabilistic selection (ACO), ordered crossover and swap mutation (GA), or continuous-to-discrete argsort mapping (GWO, HHO, MPA, SMA). Each candidate tour is evaluated by calling the inner loop, which computes the precise surface-to-surface cost.

Inner-Loop (Waypoint Refinement)

The inner loop solves the continuous geometric subproblem: for each consecutive pair of sensor spheres in the tour, where exactly should the drone land? It searches over (θ, φ) spherical coordinates on each sensor’s coverage surface to find the entry/exit points that minimise edge-to-edge Euclidean distance. Algorithms suited to continuous optimisation — PSO, DE, WOA, SSA, AHA — excel here because the landscape is smooth and low-dimensional.
